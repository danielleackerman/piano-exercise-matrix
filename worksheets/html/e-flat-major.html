Scales
01 Scale, Parallel and Contrary Motion

Begin with two octaves, parallel motion, to get familiar with how the scale feels under the hands. Extend to four octaves, then add contrary motion — one hand ascending while the other descends simultaneously.

The standard fingering follows the two-then-three pattern of the thumb: 1-2-3 and then 1-2-3-4. On the way up, the hand crosses over naturally. On the way back, the thumb crossing is where trouble starts.

Technique

On the ascent, name the finger at each position change where the 3-group meets the 4-group — "four three, four three, four three, four" — to track the landing as the hand moves up.

On the descent, name the thumb out loud at every occurrence — "first, first, first" — and accent it slightly at first to lock in the feel. The descent is where the thumb placement is hardest to track.

For speed, avoid sliding the hand in and out from the keyboard. Keep the hand rather closed than spread, active but relaxed at the same time. Being both very active and very relaxed is the difficulty of the piano; staying close to the keys makes fast playing possible.

02 Scale in Thirds

The right hand plays the scale a third above the left — starting with E♭ in the left and G in the right, a major third because the key is major.

Tip

Listen to the left hand and let the right hand match its evenness, not the other way around. The left is almost always the slower hand. Most of the unevenness appears on the descent.

Chords
03 Six Long Chords

Play six tonic chords in sequence — three inversions of E♭ major, each followed immediately by its parallel minor:

1) E♭ major — root position: E♭ – G – B♭

2) E♭ minor — root position: E♭ – G♭ – B♭

3) E♭ major — 1st inversion: G – B♭ – E♭

4) E♭ minor — 1st inversion: G♭ – B♭ – E♭

5) E♭ major — 2nd inversion: B♭ – E♭ – G

6) E♭ minor — 2nd inversion: B♭ – E♭ – G♭

Play each as a long arpeggio across the keyboard, then move immediately to the next shape in sequence.

Use white-key-style fingering across these shapes — the same hand position carried through all six chords, even where the notes land on black keys. The fingering holds constant so that the six chords can move in quick succession.

Technique

Avoid accenting the transition between chords. Most of the positions are physically uncomfortable — that discomfort is the point. Practicing these shapes hard here makes similar passages in repertoire feel easier later. Start slowly, get familiar with each shape, then increase speed.

Apply rhythmic variations from the Variations section to train the jump from one position to the next. Grouping by twos, threes, or fours forces the hand to move quickly across the chord change and exposes unevenness at the transition — the exact skill this exercise is designed to build.

04 Hand-Alternation Arpeggios, Tonic

Play the E♭ major tonic arpeggio as a single continuous line, but alternate hands across it — the goal is for four notes from two hands to sound as if one hand played them all.

Pattern, ascending through inversions:

LH — tonic, root position: E♭ – G – B♭ – E♭

RH — tonic, 1st inversion: G – B♭ – E♭ – G

LH — tonic, 1st inversion: G – B♭ – E♭ – G

RH — tonic, 2nd inversion: B♭ – E♭ – G – B♭

LH — tonic, 2nd inversion: B♭ – E♭ – G – B♭

RH — tonic, root position: E♭ – G – B♭ – E♭

Any unevenness between the hands will be audible immediately — especially at speed, where the goal is to imitate the seamless sweep of a harp.

Technique

Isolate the handoff using only the thumb and second finger of each hand:

Step 1 — Play the arpeggio using thumb and 2nd finger only in both hands.

Step 2 — Focus on the moment of exchange between hands; the change must be seamless, with no bump in time or volume.

Step 3 — Practice without pedal. Pedal masks imperfections — only dry practice reveals the truth of the transition.

05 Hand-Alternation Arpeggios, Dominant

Apply the same alternating-hand technique to the dominant chord, B♭ major — the V of E♭ major. B♭ – D – F.

Pattern follows Exercise 04: LH root, RH 1st inv, LH 1st inv, RH 2nd inv, LH 2nd inv, RH root.

Tip

The dominant version carries the same technical goal as the tonic — a seamless handoff between hands. Practice without pedal here as well.

Variations
Contrary Motion

Any scale or arpeggio exercise can be played in contrary motion — one hand ascending while the other descends simultaneously. This is harder than parallel motion and excellent brain training.

Accent & Rhythmic Variations

Apply to any scale or arpeggio exercise for fluidity and evenness:

a Accent every note — extra weight on each individual note.

b Accent every second note — grouped, rolling feel.

c Triplet groupings — organize the scale in groups of three.

d Groups of four — organize the scale in groups of four.

e Prolong one note per group — hold the 2nd or 3rd note slightly longer to build control.

f Groups of seven — the scale normally falls in groups of eight, so grouping in sevens shifts the accent and exposes unevenness.

Staccato

Any scale exercise can be played staccato — lift from each key rather than connecting the notes. Treat it as a separate pass, not a replacement for legato practice.

General Practice Principles
At an advanced stage, stay close to the keys. Lifting the fingers high is useful early on for activating and strengthening them, but once control is established, close contact is what allows speed.
Active and relaxed at the same time — that paradox is the foundation of piano technique. Neither alone is enough.
Practice without the pedal. Pedal smooths over imperfections; dry playing is what reveals them. Any passage that only sounds clean with pedal is not yet clean.
Start slowly with any new shape, then use rhythmic variations to build speed. Variations are not decoration — they are the mechanism by which fast playing is learned.

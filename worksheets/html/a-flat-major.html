Note on A♭ major

A♭ major has four flats: B♭, E♭, A♭, D♭. The hand settles into a comfortable black-key shape — despite the four flats, the key feels natural under the fingers.

Scales
01 Scale — Parallel Motion

Play A♭ major up and down in parallel motion across 4 octaves. If four octaves is too difficult, start with two and build up from there.

Tip

Right hand begins with the 2nd finger on A♭; left hand begins with the 3rd finger on A♭.

02 Scale — Contrary Motion

Play A♭ major with hands moving in opposite directions — one ascending, one descending simultaneously.

Tip

Contrary motion takes time to learn — be patient.

03 Scale in Thirds and Tenths

Thirds: the hands play parallel lines a third apart, moving together up and down the scale.

Tenths: push the right hand up an octave from its third position. The interval between the hands becomes a tenth (an octave plus a third). The right hand finishes comfortably on the 5th finger.

Tip

Once the scale feels secure, play it fast — put a small accent on every eighth note so each accent becomes an anchor you can refocus on if the hands start to drift.

04 Scale in Sixths

Move the left hand to the 6th-below position and start with the 5th finger. Play the scale with the hands a sixth apart.

Practice method — dynamic swap: increase the volume of the left hand and decrease the volume of the right. Listen for whether the hands are actually in sync — under normal dynamics, a stronger right hand masks left-hand lag. Then swap: louder right, quieter left.

Tip

Sixths are one of the most beautifully sounding intervals — take time to hear the sound.

05 Wrist Rotation — Fourths and Fifths

Play the A♭ major scale using only two fingers in an alternating rocking motion. This trains wrist rotation — a fundamental piano technique.

Fourths: both hands use thumb and 4th finger, alternating (thumb, 4, thumb, 4...) up the scale.

Fifths: both hands use thumb and 5th finger, alternating the same way.

Tip

The purpose is the rotation, not the notes. Keep the motion loose and even.

Arpeggios & Chords — Tonic
07 Arpeggios

Play A♭ – C – E♭ broken triads across 4 octaves. Work through root position, 1st inversion, and 2nd inversion.

08 Broken Chords

Play the tonic chord in broken form across the keyboard. A broken chord rearranges the chord tones out of sequential order, jumping between intervals rather than climbing straight through them.

Root position pattern: A♭ – E♭ – C – A♭ – E♭ – C – A♭ – E♭ (repeating across octaves)

Fingering (root position): RH 1-5-3-1-5-3 LH 5-1-3-5-1-3

09 Chords

Play solid chords across three or more octaves using the four-note shape A♭ – C – E♭ – A♭ (triad plus octave). Work through root position, 1st inversion, and 2nd inversion.

Warm-up method: Play just the octave (A♭ to A♭), then add the middle voices (C, E♭) one at a time. This activates the inner fingers while the octave frame stays stable.

10 Long Arpeggios

Extended arpeggios on A♭ – C – E♭ – A♭ (four-note shape, triad plus octave) across the full keyboard. Play root position, 1st inversion, and 2nd inversion.

Apply rhythmic variations to expose unevenness: prolong the 2nd note of each group, then prolong the 3rd note, then place an accent on the thumb. Prolonging one note per group forces a stop on each finger in turn — 2nd finger, thumb, 4th finger. The thumb accent is particularly useful on the descent, where the thumb-under motion normally creates problems.

Tip

Practicing with the 4th and 2nd fingers held as a paired unit helps stabilize hand shape during inversions.

Dominant — E♭7
11 E♭7 Exercises (Arpeggios, Broken Chords, Chords, Long Arpeggios)

Repeat exercises 7–10 using E♭ – G – B♭ – D♭ (V7 of A♭ major). The chord form is a four-note exercise across five fingers when the octave is added. Play through all inversions: root, 1st, 2nd, 3rd.

Warm-up: Octave first, then add the three middle voices.

Tip

Bigger stretch than the tonic. Don't play these at the start of warm-up — let the hand loosen up first.

Finishing
12 Diminished — G dim7

Built on the 7th degree of A♭ major: G – B♭ – D♭ – F♭ (fully diminished 7th chord, stacked minor thirds). This is a four-note chord exercise. Apply the same arpeggio and chord exercises. To be expanded in later sessions.

13 Chromatic Scale

Play the chromatic scale starting from A♭ across the full keyboard. A good way to close out your session.

Variations
Contrary Motion

Any scale or arpeggio exercise can be played in contrary motion — one hand ascending while the other descends simultaneously. This is harder than parallel motion and excellent brain training.

Accent & Rhythmic Variations

Apply to any scale or arpeggio exercise for fluidity and evenness:

a Accent every note — extra weight on each individual note.

b Accent every second note — grouped, rolling feel.

c Triplet groupings — organize the scale in groups of three.

d Groups of four — organize the scale in groups of four.

e Prolong one note per group — hold the 2nd or 3rd note slightly longer to build control.

f Groups of seven — the scale normally falls in groups of eight, so grouping in sevens shifts the accent and exposes unevenness.

Staccato

Any scale exercise can be played staccato — lift from each key rather than connecting the notes. Treat it as a separate pass, not a replacement for legato practice.

General Practice Principles
When playing fast, stable rhythm matters more than anything — use small accents as anchors to keep the hands from drifting across the keyboard
Listen to the left hand and match the right hand to it, not the reverse — the right hand is usually stronger
If the left hand feels uncomfortable in any exercise, practice it separately before combining
Practicing scales with added difficulty — rotation drills, Hanon, rhythmic variations — makes the simple scale feel effortless by comparison
Move the body's center with the hands across the keyboard rather than reaching with the arms alone
Pay particular attention to the 4th and 2nd fingers — these are usually the weakest and benefit from accents and deliberate stops

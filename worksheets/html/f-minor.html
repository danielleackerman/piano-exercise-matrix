A note on F minor

F minor carries four flats: A♭, B♭, D♭, E♭. Practice the harmonic and melodic forms, not just the natural minor.

Harmonic minor raises the 7th degree — E♭ becomes E natural. This creates the augmented-second interval between the 6th and 7th degrees that gives the harmonic minor its character.

Melodic minor raises both the 6th and 7th degrees ascending — D♭ becomes D natural, E♭ becomes E natural. Descending, the scale reverts to natural minor with all four flats restored.

Scales
01 Scale, Parallel Motion

Play F harmonic minor and F melodic minor up and down in parallel motion across 4 octaves. These are the foundation — fluency in the single-note scale is a prerequisite for double-octave work below.

Tip

Take the harmonic form first, then the melodic, before moving on to double octaves.

Double Octaves
02 Scale in Double Octaves

Play F harmonic minor and F melodic minor in double octaves across 3–4 octaves. Each hand plays the scale across its own octave span, thumb and 5th finger on every note.

On black keys in both hands, prefer the 4th finger over the 5th. The 4th finger sits more naturally on a raised key and keeps the hand freer. The 5th works, but the 4th is more comfortable.

Technique

Relaxation is everything. Locked fingers and a locked arm will fatigue within seconds. Two drills build the right feel:

Step 1 — Hot-key jump. Imagine the keys are burning hot. Touch and release immediately, bouncing off the surface rather than pressing into it.

Step 2 — Fist release. After playing each octave, make a fist with the hand. Play, fist, play, fist. This interrupts the habit of locking in one position.

The wrist must stay flexible — a small motion slightly up, then slightly down, exaggerated at first until the looseness becomes natural. A rigid wrist causes tension that cannot be undone mid-scale.

03 Sustained Octaves

Instead of jumping, press each octave and hold it slightly, then release fully before the next. Press and relax. Press and relax.

Tip

The opposite awareness to the hot-key jump — releasing tension between notes rather than during them. Both approaches train the same underlying goal: never stay locked.

04 Balance the Voicing

Double octaves sound four notes simultaneously — thumb and 5th in each hand. Unevenness between these four voices is common, and one voice tends to get swallowed, most often a thumb. Train each voice in isolation:

a Play the full scale, bringing out the left-hand 5th finger — increase its volume while keeping the other three voices quieter.

b Play the full scale, bringing out the left-hand thumb.

c Play the full scale, bringing out the right-hand thumb.

d Play the full scale, bringing out the right-hand 5th finger.

Tip

The goal is four notes sounding almost equally. Voicing emphasis after that is a musical choice, not an accident.

05 Scale in Double Octaves, Contrary Motion

Play F harmonic minor in double octaves with hands moving in opposite directions — one ascending while the other descends simultaneously.

Tip

Useful for the brain as much as the hands. The two hands encounter different black-key patterns at different moments, which breaks any reliance on mirrored muscle memory.

06 Tonic Long Arpeggios in Double Octaves

Play the F minor tonic arpeggio in double octaves across the keyboard, through its inversions:

Root position: F – A♭ – C – F

1st inversion: A♭ – C – F – A♭

2nd inversion: C – F – A♭ – C

The earlier scale drills trained moving from one note to the next. This exercise trains changing position — remembering the interval distances between octaves. Between the notes of the tonic triad, the hand alternates between a minor third, a major third, and a fourth. Those changing spans are the technical work.

Technique

If jumps feel unreliable, shrink the distances first: practice with small jumps within a single octave before extending the full range across the keyboard. Speed exposes weakness — a passage that sounds clean slow may fall apart fast. Build tempo bit by bit rather than attempting full speed and losing accuracy.

07 Thumb-Only Octaves

Play the scale using only the thumb of each hand, ignoring the 5th finger entirely. A single-voice version of the double-octave scale, left and right hands each playing one line.

Tip

Isolates the thumb, which is usually the weaker voice in octave playing. Especially useful for the left hand, which tends to feel weaker across the full octave span.

08 VII Diminished in Double Octaves

Built on the 7th degree of F harmonic minor, E natural. The diminished triad is E – G – B♭, each interval a minor third.

Play this diminished chord as an arpeggio in double octaves across at least three octaves, through its inversions.

Tip

Apply rhythmic variations from the Variations section — stopping on every second, third, or fourth note — to expose unevenness within the arpeggio. Combine with the thumb-only approach from Exercise 07 for additional isolation.

Chords
09 Tonic Chord, Full Sound

Play the F minor tonic chord as a solid chord, through its inversions, with the goal of producing a rich, projected tone rather than simply striking the notes.

Root position: F – A♭ – C – F

1st inversion: A♭ – C – F – A♭

2nd inversion: C – F – A♭ – C

Technique

The same rule as double octaves applies: do not lock the hand. Spreading the fingers and reaching with the fingers alone produces a thin, pushed sound no matter how hard the keys are struck. A full sound requires the whole arm — elbow, shoulder, and upper back all engaged, with a connected and flexible wrist.

To check for lockup, rest on the chord and try to swing the elbow freely. If the elbow cannot swing, the arm is locked and the tone will suffer. Release until the swing is easy, then play.

If the full voicing is too wide for the hand, use a smaller one — three notes rather than four. Either works. For fingering on the top note, either 5 or 4 is acceptable; choose whichever is more comfortable.

Variations
Contrary Motion

Any scale or arpeggio exercise can be played in contrary motion — one hand ascending while the other descends simultaneously. This is harder than parallel motion and excellent brain training.

Accent & Rhythmic Variations

Apply to any scale or arpeggio exercise for fluidity and evenness:

a Accent every note — extra weight on each individual note.

b Accent every second note — grouped, rolling feel.

c Triplet groupings — organize the scale in groups of three.

d Groups of four — organize the scale in groups of four.

e Prolong one note per group — hold the 2nd or 3rd note slightly longer to build control.

f Groups of seven — the scale normally falls in groups of eight, so grouping in sevens shifts the accent and exposes unevenness.

Staccato

Any scale exercise can be played staccato — lift from each key rather than connecting the notes. Treat it as a separate pass, not a replacement for legato practice.

General Practice Principles
Octaves and chords share one foundation: whole-arm connection with a free, flexible wrist. The fingers alone cannot produce sound that carries.
Octave work is a balance between staying on the key and leaving it quickly. Both habits must be available; neither can become the only mode.
Fast practice reveals what slow practice hides. If accuracy collapses at speed, the slow version was not yet solid.

Scales
01 Scale — Parallel & Contrary Motion

Play B♭ major in two octaves first, then expand to four. The scale starts on a black key, so the standard fifth-finger / thumb opening doesn't work — the fingering is unusual.

Right hand: 2 – 1 – 3 – 1 – 2 – 3 – 1 – 4 (ascending; reverse the pattern descending)

Left hand: 3 – 2 – 1 – 4 – 3 – 2 – 1

Once the parallel motion is fluid, add contrary motion — hands moving in opposite directions, one ascending while the other descends simultaneously.

Tip

B♭ major is one of the most comfortable keys for warm-up once the fingering is in the hand. Start slow, prioritize evenness over speed.

02 Scale in Sixths

Play the scale in the interval of a sixth between the hands. The right hand carries the top voice on B♭; the left hand plays a sixth below — C in the octave below — and both hands move together up the scale.

03 Double Sixths

Activating the 3rd, 4th, and 5th fingers.

The 3rd, 4th, and 5th fingers are the laziest fingers on the hand and cause most of the unevenness in scale playing. This exercise activates them directly. One hand at a time. Use only B♭ major notes throughout. Practice in two octaves.

The same fingering is used the entire way up and down — no adjustments, no shortcuts. The aim is twofold: to build strength in the weakest fingers and to teach them to carry a legato line on their own.

a — Left hand, alternating 5–1 / 4–1

The left hand plays both notes of the sixth simultaneously, using two fingers at a time. The thumb (1) always plays the top note. The bottom note alternates between the 5th and the 4th finger as the hand moves up the scale. Starting position: 5 on the low B♭, thumb on the G a sixth above. As the hand moves up, the bottom finger swaps to 4 on the next sixth, then back to 5, and so on. The fifth finger will land on a black key at points — this is normal and is part of the activation.

b — Right hand, alternating 1–4 / 1–5

Same idea on the right hand, with the thumb now on the bottom. The thumb (1) always plays the lower note of the sixth. The top note alternates between the 4th and 5th finger. Starting position: thumb on B♭, 5 on the G a sixth above; the next sixth uses 1 paired with 4, then 1 with 5, and so on. Play legato — keep the top voice connected.

If sounding the thumb at the same time as the upper finger feels too complicated at first, the thumb can be omitted so only the top voice (4 and 5 alternating) is played. The thumb is reintroduced once the upper-finger pattern is secure.

The thumb must stay relaxed throughout. The hand should feel divided into two parts — the thumb side and the 3–4–5 side — moving independently of each other rather than as a single unit.

c — Add the 3rd finger

Repeat (a) and (b), now bringing the 3rd finger into the pattern. The thumb still anchors one note of every sixth in both hands; the other note now alternates across 3, 4, and 5 instead of just 4 and 5. Same rule: one fingering all the way up and down.

d — Stop on the 4th finger

A control drill. Play the 3, 4, 5 pattern but place a small stop on every 4th finger — the pause gives the hand time to think about the fingering and to release tension before moving on. Sounds harder than it is; it's actually a relaxation tool.

e — Finger substitution on the same note

Change fingers on the same note while holding it down — a stairstepping motion across 4 and 5 on a single pitch. Hold the note with 4, swap to 5 without releasing, then continue the pattern up the scale. Practice each hand separately. This is how legato is achieved in passages where the hand cannot physically connect the notes by stretching, and it shows up constantly in repertoire.

f — Combined: 3-4-5 legato, thumb staccato

Once finger substitution is secure, combine it with the thumb. Two articulations happen at the same time: 5–4–3 stays legato while the thumb plays staccato. The hand is literally doing two different things at once — that independence is the goal of the entire exercise.

g — Split sixths in rotation

A variation of the same physical action. Instead of striking both notes of the sixth together, split them — bottom note, then top note, in a continuous rotation movement of the forearm. The sixth becomes broken rather than solid, and the wrist and forearm do most of the work.

h — Increase the speed

Once the patterns are secure, push the tempo. The goal of finger substitution and of all the drills above is to make the change as fast as possible. The left hand will be lazier than the right — expect it, work it harder.

Variations
Contrary Motion

Any scale or arpeggio exercise can be played in contrary motion — one hand ascending while the other descends simultaneously. Harder than parallel motion and excellent brain training.

Accent & Rhythmic Variations

Apply to any scale or arpeggio exercise for fluidity and evenness:

a Accent every note — extra weight on each individual note.

b Accent every second note — grouped, rolling feel.

c Triplet groupings — organize the scale in groups of three.

d Groups of four — organize the scale in groups of four.

e Prolong one note per group — hold the 2nd or 3rd note slightly longer to build control.

f Groups of seven — the scale normally falls in groups of eight, so grouping in sevens shifts the accent and exposes unevenness.

Staccato

Any scale exercise can be played staccato — lift from each key rather than connecting the notes. Treat it as a separate pass, not a replacement for legato practice.

General Practice Principles
B♭ major scales open on a black key, which forces an unusual fingering — the standard fifth-finger / thumb opening does not apply
Once the fingering is in the hand, B♭ major is one of the most comfortable keys for daily warm-up
The 3rd, 4th, and 5th fingers are the laziest fingers on the hand — most unevenness in scale playing traces back to them
Finger substitution on a single note is the way to achieve legato where the hand can't physically connect notes by stretching — useful in scales and indispensable in repertoire
The hand should feel divided into two independent halves — the thumb side and the 3-4-5 side — moving independently of each other rather than as a single unit

A note on minor scales

Practice the harmonic and melodic forms, not the natural minor. The natural minor in C uses E♭, A♭, B♭ throughout. The harmonic minor raises the 7th: B becomes B natural. The melodic minor raises both the 6th and 7th ascending — A becomes A natural, B becomes B natural — and reverts to natural minor descending (B♭, A♭ on the way down).

Scales
01 Harmonic Minor — Parallel Motion

Play C harmonic minor up and down with both hands moving in the same direction. The harmonic minor raises the 7th degree: B is natural, not B♭.

Tip

Incorporate dynamic shaping. Begin softer, increase the volume gradually, then taper back down to piano. Scales are not just finger work — train tone control at the same time.

02 Harmonic Minor — Contrary Motion

Play C harmonic minor with hands moving in opposite directions — one ascending, one descending simultaneously.

03 Melodic Minor — Parallel Motion

Ascending: raise both the 6th and 7th degrees — A natural, B natural.

Descending: revert to natural minor — B♭, A♭ on the way back down (C – B♭ – A♭ – G – F – E♭ – D – C).

04 Melodic Minor — Contrary Motion

Play C melodic minor in contrary motion.

05 Scale in Tenths

Play the melodic minor scale in tenths — the right hand plays one octave higher than the third position between the hands. Try both ascending and descending.

Arpeggios & Chords — Tonic
06 Chords

Play the C minor tonic chord as solid four-note chords: C – E♭ – G – C (triad plus octave). The octave frame stabilizes the hand while the inner voices (E♭, G) activate the middle fingers. Practice across the keyboard with inversions.

Technique

Think about flexible wrists. By the end of the chord work the hand should know the shape — that familiarity carries directly into the arpeggios.

07 Chords + Octaves Combined

Alternate between the full four-note chord and the bare octave in continuous motion: chord down, octave up, chord down, octave up — repeating across the keyboard. The chord and the octave are physically different shapes, and switching between them under tempo trains both the grip and the release.

08 Arpeggios

Play C minor arpeggios — C – E♭ – G — broken across 4 octaves in root position and inversions.

09 Broken Arpeggios

Play the chord tones out of sequential order rather than climbing straight through them. Root position pattern: C – G – E♭ – C – G – E♭ — repeating across octaves.

Tip

The first inversion starts from the black key (E♭) and is always slightly uncomfortable. Practice it in tempo to expose unevenness — the left hand especially. If unevenness shows up, isolate the left hand and work it alone before recombining.

10 Long Arpeggios

Extended arpeggios across the full keyboard. Root position, 1st inversion, 2nd inversion.

Technique

The thumb-to-third-finger crossing is the choke point. Three drills target it directly:

Step 1 — Take the note immediately before the crossing and play it together with the third finger faster, then stop on the third finger to feel a moment of relaxation.

Step 2 — On the first inversion, prolong the first note of each group. The held first note forces the thumb-to-third crossing to happen quickly, because the change is where the unevenness lives.

Step 3 — On the last inversion, remove the two middle notes and leave only the thumb and third finger sounding — but place the rest of the hand in position as if the full dominant chord were going to be played next.

The point of all three drills is preparation. Missed notes happen when the hand arrives late. These drills train the hand to be ready before it needs to be.

Dominant — G7
11 G7 Exercises (Chords, Arpeggios, Broken Arpeggios, Long Arpeggios)

Repeat exercises 6, 8, 9, and 10 using G – B – D – F (V7 of C minor). Play through all inversions: root, 1st, 2nd, 3rd. The dominant in C minor is built on the same notes as the dominant in C major — the V7 chord pulls B natural regardless of the minor key signature.

Finishing
12 Diminished — B°7

Built on the raised 7th degree of the harmonic minor: B – D – F – A♭ (stacked minor thirds, four notes). Apply the same arpeggio and chord exercises. To be expanded in later sessions.

13 Chromatic Scale

Play the chromatic scale starting from C across the full keyboard. A good way to close out the session.

Variations
Contrary Motion

Any scale or arpeggio exercise can be played in contrary motion — one hand ascending while the other descends simultaneously. Harder than parallel motion and excellent brain training.

Accent & Rhythmic Variations

Apply to any scale or arpeggio exercise for fluidity and evenness:

a Accent every note — extra weight on each individual note.

b Accent every second note — grouped, rolling feel.

c Triplet groupings — organize the scale in groups of three.

d Groups of four — organize the scale in groups of four.

e Prolong one note per group — hold the 2nd or 3rd note slightly longer to build control.

f Groups of seven — the scale normally falls in groups of eight, so grouping in sevens shifts the accent and exposes unevenness.

Staccato

Any scale exercise can be played staccato — lift from each key rather than connecting the notes. Treat it as a separate pass, not a replacement for legato practice.

General Practice Principles
C minor is the most comfortable minor key to start with; the three flats give the fingers black-key landmarks that all-white-key minor scales lack
Many things in C minor work exactly the same as in C major — the dominant chord, the diminished chord, and the chromatic scale are shared between the two keys
The harmonic minor and melodic minor forms are what give minor scales their technical value; the natural minor is acoustically identical to the relative major and trains nothing new
Dynamic shaping belongs inside scale practice, not separate from it — train tone and finger control on the same pass
Unevenness shows up first in the left hand; play in tempo to expose it, then isolate the left hand to fix it
Most missed notes are preparation failures, not finger failures — the hand arrived at the new position too late

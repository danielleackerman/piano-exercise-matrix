D MAJOR SCALE PRACTICE WORKSHEET
==========================================================

KEY: D Major
RANGE: Always aim for 4 octaves across the full keyboard.

KEY SIGNATURE: 2 sharps — F♯, C♯

D major introduces playing from black keys. F♯ and C♯ serve as landmarks
and the scale sits comfortably under the hand once the positions are familiar.


----------------------------------------------------------
SCALES
----------------------------------------------------------

1. SCALE — PARALLEL MOTION
Play D major up and down across 4 octaves, both hands moving in the same
direction. Begin in one octave to warm the hands before extending to four.

----------------------------------------------------------

2. SCALE — CONTRARY MOTION
Same scale, hands moving in opposite directions simultaneously. Both hands
begin on D, one ascending and one descending.

----------------------------------------------------------

3. SCALE IN THIRDS
Play the D major scale in intervals of a third — two notes a third apart,
both hands together.

  Tip: Isolate the left hand if it feels unclear. Do not combine hands
  until each hand is stable alone.

----------------------------------------------------------

4. SCALE IN TENTHS
Play the scale in tenths — an octave plus a third between hands — both
hands together. Practice both ascending and descending.

----------------------------------------------------------

5. SCALE IN SIXTHS
Play the D major scale in sixths — notes a sixth apart in each hand
simultaneously. Practice in triplets.

  Tip: When practicing slowly, lift the fingers slightly higher than you
  would at speed. At full speed, return to staying close to the keyboard.

----------------------------------------------------------

6. OCTAVES — ACCENT AND RHYTHMIC VARIATIONS
Play the scale in octaves. Apply accent groupings progressively.

  a  Accent every note — deliberate weight on each octave.
  b  Accent every second note — a grouped, rolling feel.
  c  Triplet groupings — organize the octaves in threes.
  d  Groups of four — accent every fourth octave.

  Then isolate: practice using only the thumb, then only the 5th finger.

  Dynamic shaping: begin quietly, crescendo to forte at the midpoint,
  then return to piano on the way down.

  Tip: Singing along while you play reinforces rhythm and phrasing.


----------------------------------------------------------
ARPEGGIOS AND CHORDS — TONIC
----------------------------------------------------------

7. ARPEGGIOS — SHORT
Play D – F♯ – A broken triads across 4 octaves. Root position, 1st
inversion, 2nd inversion. Both hands.

----------------------------------------------------------

8. CHORDS — WARM-UP
Play solid four-note D major chords. Octave first, then add the middle
voices one at a time.

  Technique: Engage the upper back for tone. Arms alone produce a thin,
  pushed sound.

----------------------------------------------------------

9. BROKEN CHORDS
Play D – F♯ – A – D in broken form across the keyboard, through all
inversions. Use the 4th finger where the stretch would otherwise be too large.

  Tip: All notes must be equal in weight. The thumb tends to accent —
  listen for it and correct deliberately.

----------------------------------------------------------

10. LONG ARPEGGIOS
Extended arpeggios D – F♯ – A – D – F♯ – A – D across the full keyboard.
Root position, 1st inversion, 2nd inversion. Add contrary motion once stable.
Separate hands before combining.

  Fingering note for F♯: in the 1st inversion, the left hand plays the
  3rd finger on F♯, the right hand plays the 4th. Identify this in each
  inversion and give it extra attention before combining hands.

  Tip: To correct a lazy or inaccurate finger, practice with a deliberate
  accent and prolonged note on that finger only until it responds reliably.
  When tired, slow down — fatigue destroys legato.


----------------------------------------------------------
SIX-CHORD SEQUENCE
----------------------------------------------------------

11. SIX-CHORD SEQUENCE
Practice the full sequence as arpeggios, broken chords, solid chords, and
long arpeggios. Always finish on D.

  1) D major — I   (root position)
  2) D minor — i   (root position)
  3) B major — VI  (1st inversion)
  4) B minor — vi  (1st inversion)
  5) G major — IV  (2nd inversion)
  6) G minor — iv  (2nd inversion)


----------------------------------------------------------
DOMINANT — A7
----------------------------------------------------------

12. A7 EXERCISES (Arpeggios, Broken Chords, Chords, Long Arpeggios)
Repeat exercises 7–10 using A – C♯ – E – G (V7 of D major). All inversions:
root, 1st, 2nd, 3rd. Octave first, then add inner voices one at a time.

  Tip: The four-note stretch is larger than the tonic triad. Do not play
  dominant seventh exercises cold.


----------------------------------------------------------
FINISHING
----------------------------------------------------------

13. CHROMATIC SCALE
Play the chromatic scale starting from D across the full keyboard. Extend
by playing in different intervals: octaves, thirds, sixths, or tenths.


----------------------------------------------------------
GENERAL PRACTICE PRINCIPLES
----------------------------------------------------------

— Allow imperfections when the hands are cold. Start slow — evenness
  matters more than speed at the outset.

— Slow and fast practice train different things. Always include fast
  practice — it builds stamina that slow practice cannot. Push the tempo,
  or use a metronome.

— Practice separate hands before combining for arpeggios and inversions.
  Combining too early hides individual weaknesses.

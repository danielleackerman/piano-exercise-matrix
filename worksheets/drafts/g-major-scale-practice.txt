G MAJOR SCALE PRACTICE WORKSHEET
Source: Piano Buff's 24 Days Scales Challenge – Day 3
==========================================================

KEY: G Major (one sharp — F♯)
RANGE: Always aim for 4 octaves across the full keyboard.


----------------------------------------------------------
1. SCALE – PARALLEL MOTION
----------------------------------------------------------
Play G major scale up and down in parallel motion (both hands moving in the same direction).

Tip: Don't start too fast. You want to warm up and get used to the keyboard first. Start slow, prioritize evenness over speed.


----------------------------------------------------------
2. SCALE – VOICING VARIATIONS
----------------------------------------------------------
Play the G major scale while deliberately shifting the dynamic balance between hands:

a) Push the left hand louder than the right — voice out the left hand so it's not passive.
b) Push the right hand louder than the left — focus attention on the right hand tone.

Tip: Voicing practice keeps scales from becoming mindless repetition. Listen to each hand independently while both are playing.


----------------------------------------------------------
3. SCALE IN THIRDS
----------------------------------------------------------
Play the G major scale in intervals of a third (two notes a third apart, played together or in succession).

If the left hand feels awkward, isolate it — practice left hand alone before combining.


----------------------------------------------------------
4. SCALE IN SIXTHS
----------------------------------------------------------
Play the G major scale in intervals of a sixth (two notes a sixth apart, played together or in succession).

Tip: Sixths are wider than thirds and require more hand awareness. Keep the wrist flexible and let the arm guide the position shifts.


----------------------------------------------------------
5. SCALE – CONTRARY MOTION
----------------------------------------------------------
Play G major with hands moving in opposite directions — one ascending, one descending simultaneously.

Simplification method (if 4 octaves is too hard):
- Start with one octave, one hand alone. Learn the thumb-to-third-finger connection.
- Then try two octaves, one hand alone, at a very slow tempo.
- Then combine both hands in contrary motion, but only one octave.
- Then two octaves with both hands.
- Build outward from there.

Tip: You can always minimize the requirements. Don't be intimidated by the full four-octave version — work up to it gradually.


----------------------------------------------------------
6. ACCENT / RHYTHMIC VARIATIONS (for scales)
----------------------------------------------------------
Use these to improve fluidity and evenness:

a) Accent every single note — apply extra weight to each note individually.
b) Accent every second note — creates a grouped, rolling feel.
c) Triplet groupings — practice the scale organized in groups of three.

Apply these accent patterns to any scale exercise above.

Important: Keep a very clear rhythmical formula when playing scales. Without a clear rhythmic structure, you lose the passage — you touch wrong keys because you didn't prepare fast enough.


----------------------------------------------------------
7. ARPEGGIOS (Tonic – G major: G-B-D)
----------------------------------------------------------
Play broken triads (G-B-D) across 4 octaves, with inversions.

Technique tips:
- Feel the natural weight of your arm dropping into the keys.
- Move in circular motions — stay grounded and free, not rigid.
- Increase speed gradually.


----------------------------------------------------------
8. BROKEN CHORDS (Tonic – G major)
----------------------------------------------------------
Play the tonic chord in broken form (root position: G-B-D) across the keyboard.

If it feels uncomfortable:
- Practice left hand separately first.
- The left hand naturally wants to lean right on the keyboard — be aware of this pull.


----------------------------------------------------------
9. CHORDS (Tonic – G major)
----------------------------------------------------------
Play solid chords (all notes struck together), with inversions.

Warm-up method:
- First play just the octave (G to G).
- Then add the middle voices (B, D) one at a time.
- This activates the inner fingers while the octave frame stays stable.


----------------------------------------------------------
10. LONG ARPEGGIOS (Tonic – G major)
----------------------------------------------------------
Play extended arpeggios across the full keyboard.

Practice method:
- Separate hands first — this exposes weaknesses you hide when both hands play together.
- Train the left hand independently.
- Excellent exercise for training the thumb crossing.

Variations:
- Root position, 1st inversion, 2nd inversion
- Contrary motion (hands moving in opposite directions)


----------------------------------------------------------
11. DIMINISHED CHORD EXERCISES (7th degree: F♯ diminished — F♯-A-C)
----------------------------------------------------------
Built on the 7th degree of G major (F♯), using all minor thirds: F♯-A-C.

This chord stretches all muscles in the hand. It trains the expansion of the arm and builds stamina through sustained practice.

If the full chord with added octave is too difficult (especially for smaller hands), play it without the octave — just the three-note chord. All minor thirds.

a) CHORDS:
Play solid diminished chords across the keyboard with inversions. Keep a very relaxed back. Think about flexible wrists — don't lock your hands. Don't play only from the fingers. Always use the whole arm.

b) ARPEGGIOS:
Play broken triads of the diminished chord across the keyboard. Use variations to improve quality: prolong the first note, relax on it, then play the remaining notes. Then prolong the second note instead. Relaxing on prolonged notes trains you to release tension between movements.

c) BROKEN CHORDS:
Play the diminished chord in broken form across the keyboard. This teaches you to expand the arm, incorporate the wrist, and stay close to the keyboard. Practice with rhythmic variations (e.g., long-short-long-short patterns). Always practice separate hands — the left hand is weaker, especially the 3rd and 4th fingers in this stretched position.

d) LONG ARPEGGIOS:
Play extended diminished arpeggios across the full keyboard.

Fingering rule: Always put the thumb on the last white key. In G major's diminished chord, the thumb goes on C. In the right hand, the thumb goes on the first white key.

Play through all inversions: root, 1st, 2nd, 3rd. Add contrary motion.

Increase speed gradually. When you don't prepare fast enough, you touch other keys — it's all about good preparation.

e) OCTAVE VARIATION:
Play the diminished chord in octaves: 4th finger on the black key (F♯), 5th finger on the white key. Use a clear rhythmic formula (e.g., triplets) to maintain accuracy.

f) WARM-UP VARIATION:
Play octaves first, then activate the middle fingers one at a time. This combines octave stability with inner-voice activation.


----------------------------------------------------------
12. DOMINANT CHORD EXERCISES (D7: D-F♯-A-C)
----------------------------------------------------------
Repeat exercises 7–10 using the dominant 7th chord (V7 of G major = D7).

The dominant 7th requires a bigger hand stretch (4 notes instead of 3). Don't play these at the very start of your warm-up — hands need to be loose first.

Play through all inversions: root, 1st, 2nd, 3rd.

Apply the same rhythmic/accent variations from Exercise 6.


----------------------------------------------------------
13. CHROMATIC SCALE
----------------------------------------------------------
Play the chromatic scale starting from G across the full keyboard.

A good finishing exercise to close out a practice session.


==========================================================
GENERAL PRACTICE PRINCIPLES
==========================================================

- Don't start too fast — warm up first, get used to the keyboard.
- Separate hands is the savior from any problem — especially the left hand.
- Keep a very clear rhythmical formula — without clear rhythm, you lose the passage.
- Everything is about preparation — bounce back from the key and prepare for the next position before you arrive.
- Use the whole arm, not just the fingers. Keep wrists flexible, back relaxed. Don't lock your hands.
- Stay close to the keyboard, especially during broken chords and arpeggios.
- You can always minimize the requirements — don't be intimidated, make it easier and build up.
- Scales practice is about creativity — find variations, set goals, make it faster, more even, try new patterns.
- Stamina matters — when your muscles start to burn, the exercise is doing its job.
- Voice out each hand separately — push left hand volume, then right hand volume, to keep both hands engaged.
- Use variations to train relaxation — prolong a note, relax on it, then continue.

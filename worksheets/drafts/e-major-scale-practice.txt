SCALES
============================================================

01 — Scale

Play E major across 4 octaves. Start with one octave if needed,
then expand. Choose a comfortable tempo.


02 — Scale in Tenths

Play E major in tenths (octave + third between hands). Add
dynamics: crescendo ascending, decrescendo descending.

Practice left hand separately — avoid any spikes or unintended
accents on individual notes.


03 — Scale in Sixths

Play E major in sixths. Start with G♯ in the left hand. Apply
accent patterns from the Variations section.


============================================================
CHROMATIC SCALE IN DOUBLE THIRDS
============================================================

04 — Chromatic Scale in Double Thirds

Chromatic scales are more or less the same from all keys. Start
from C using major thirds. The most important thing is knowing
where your fingers go — specifically where the 5th finger lands.

5th finger anchor points:
  RH — 5th finger on F and A♯ (top notes of those pairs)
  LH — 5th finger on F♯ and B (bottom notes of those pairs)

Complete fingering, ascending from C (bottom note / top note):

  RH:
  C-E: 1/4    C♯-F: 2/5    D-F♯: 1/3    D♯-G: 2/4
  E-G♯: 1/3   F-A: 1/4     F♯-A♯: 2/5   G-B: 1/3
  G♯-C: 2/4

  LH:
  C-E: 4/1    C♯-F: 3/1    D-F♯: 4/2    D♯-G: 3/1
  E-G♯: 4/2   F-A: 3/1     F♯-A♯: 5/2   G-B: 4/1
  G♯-C: 3/1   A-C♯: 4/2    A♯-D: 3/1    B-D♯: 5/2

Both hands use almost the same pattern — a mirror effect.
Practice separate hands first. Connect each pair of thirds as
legato as possible. Then combine both hands very slowly.

  TIP
  Say the 5th-finger note names out loud as you practice:
  "F, A♯, A♯, F" (RH ascending then descending). This helps
  lock in the anchor points.

  NOTE
  You can also play chromatic double thirds using minor thirds.
  Major thirds are used here because we are playing E major.


============================================================
DOUBLE OCTAVES
============================================================

05 — Double Octaves

Play E major in double octaves. Apply triplet groupings from the
Variations section. Start slowly, then increase the tempo.

  TECHNIQUE
  Don't play with locked hands — this creates tension immediately.
  Keep the wrists free. Jump from each key and play staccato. To
  learn how to bounce, the wrist must be relaxed.


06 — Double Octave Arpeggios

Play arpeggios in double octaves. Add contrary motion.

  Practice method: Stop on every single note to check accuracy.
  Then connect pairs of notes. The distances vary greatly between
  jumps — this is what makes it a great exercise for practicing
  jumps.

  TIP
  Practice the outer voices (top and bottom notes only) before
  playing full double octaves.


============================================================
CHORDS
============================================================

07 — Chords

Play solid chords with inversions. Start with a smaller chord
to locate the position, then expand to the full voicing.

  Practice method (from Vienna): Divide the chord into pairs of
  two notes and practice pressing them together simultaneously.
  The 3rd finger in the left hand often arrives sooner than the
  5th finger, creating unevenness — the chord sounds like the
  fingers are not together. Practicing in pairs eliminates this.

  Variation: Use three-note groups instead of pairs.

  Middle voices exercise: Play the outer voices (octave frame)
  staccato, then add the middle voices. For variety, try an
  E major 7th chord. If the stretch is too big, remove one voice.

VARIATIONS

(unnumbered card)
Title: Contrary Motion

Any scale or arpeggio exercise can be played in contrary motion —
one hand ascending while the other descends simultaneously. This
is harder than parallel motion and excellent brain training.


(unnumbered card)
Title: Accent & Rhythmic Variations

Apply to any scale or arpeggio exercise for fluidity and evenness:

  a  Accent every second note — grouped, rolling feel.
  b  Triplet groupings — organize in groups of three.
  c  Dynamics — crescendo ascending, decrescendo descending.

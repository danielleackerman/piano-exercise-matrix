C MAJOR SCALE PRACTICE WORKSHEET
Source: Piano Buff's 24 Days Scales Challenge – Day 1
==========================================================

KEY: C Major (no sharps or flats)
RANGE: Always aim for 4 octaves across the full keyboard.


----------------------------------------------------------
1. SCALE – PARALLEL MOTION
----------------------------------------------------------
Play C major scale up and down in parallel motion (both hands moving in the same direction).

Fingering reminder:
- 4th finger falls on D
- 3rd finger falls on A
- Because there are no black keys, fingering is actually tricky — you lose the tactile landmarks that sharps/flats provide.

Tip: Start slow, then increase speed. Prioritize even tone over tempo.


----------------------------------------------------------
2. SCALE – CONTRARY MOTION
----------------------------------------------------------
Play the same C major scale with hands moving in opposite directions (one ascending, one descending simultaneously).

Tip: This is harder than parallel motion and is excellent brain training. Add contrary motion to any exercise below for an extra challenge.


----------------------------------------------------------
3. SCALE IN THIRDS
----------------------------------------------------------
Play the C major scale in intervals of a third (two notes a third apart, played together or in succession).

If the left hand feels awkward, isolate it — practice left hand alone before combining.


----------------------------------------------------------
4. ACCENT / RHYTHMIC VARIATIONS (for scales)
----------------------------------------------------------
Use these to improve fluidity and evenness:

a) Accent every single note – apply extra weight to each note individually.
b) Accent every second note – creates a grouped, rolling feel.
c) Triplet groupings – practice the scale organized in groups of three.

Apply these accent patterns to any scale exercise above.


----------------------------------------------------------
5. SCALE IN TENTHS (using triplets)
----------------------------------------------------------
Play the scale in tenths (an octave + a third between the hands) using triplet rhythm.

Note: This is significantly harder. Practice ascending AND descending — don't just go bottom to top.


----------------------------------------------------------
6. ARPEGGIOS (Tonic – C major: C-E-G)
----------------------------------------------------------
Play broken triads across 4 octaves.

Technique tips:
- Feel the natural weight of your arm dropping into the keys.
- Move in circular motions — stay grounded and free, not rigid.
- Increase speed gradually.


----------------------------------------------------------
7. BROKEN CHORDS (Tonic – C major)
----------------------------------------------------------
Play the tonic chord in broken form (root position: C-E-G) across the keyboard.

If it feels uncomfortable:
- Practice left hand separately first.
- The left hand naturally wants to lean right on the keyboard — be aware of this.


----------------------------------------------------------
8. CHORDS (Tonic – C major)
----------------------------------------------------------
Play solid chords (all notes struck together).

Warm-up method (especially for cold hands):
- First play just the OCTAVE (C to C).
- Then add the middle voices (E, G) one at a time.
- This activates the inner fingers while the octave frame stays stable.

Technique tips:
- Lock in the octave feel — octaves always feel the same physically.
- Activate your back muscles, not just your arms.
- Playing from the arms alone produces a pushed, thin sound.
- Engage the upper back to produce a heavy, beautiful tone.
- Don't lift your shoulders.


----------------------------------------------------------
9. LONG ARPEGGIOS (Tonic – C major)
----------------------------------------------------------
Play extended arpeggios across the full keyboard.

Practice method:
- Separate hands first — this exposes weaknesses you hide when both hands play together.
- The right hand typically covers for left hand insecurities. Train the left hand independently.
- This is an excellent exercise for training the thumb crossing.

Variations:
- Root position, 1st inversion, 2nd inversion
- Contrary motion (hands moving in opposite directions)

Technique tips:
- Move the center of your body with your hands instead of reaching with arms alone.
- Sit on the edge of the chair — sitting too far back puts your weight into the chair instead of into the keyboard.


----------------------------------------------------------
10. DOMINANT CHORD EXERCISES (G7: G-B-D-F)
----------------------------------------------------------
Repeat exercises 6–9 using the dominant 7th chord (V7 of C major = G7).

Why it matters:
- The G7 chord requires a bigger hand stretch (4 notes instead of 3).
- Don't play these at the very start of your warm-up — hands need to be loose first.

Warm-up method (same principle as tonic chords):
- Play the octave first.
- Then add the three middle voices — even more stretch than before.

Play through all inversions: root, 1st, 2nd, 3rd.

Difficulty note:
- Some notes are close together (F and G), then there's a bigger gap — this makes it physically awkward.
- Every hand is different; adjust as needed.

Apply the same rhythmic/accent variations from Exercise 4.


----------------------------------------------------------
11. DIMINISHED CHORD (on 7th degree: B diminished)
----------------------------------------------------------
Built on the 7th degree of C major (B), using all minor thirds: B-D-F.

(Not covered in detail in this session — to be expanded later.)


----------------------------------------------------------
12. CHROMATIC SCALE
----------------------------------------------------------
Play the chromatic scale starting from C across the full keyboard.

A good finishing exercise to close out a practice session.


==========================================================
GENERAL PRACTICE PRINCIPLES
==========================================================

- Always play in 4 octaves for serious practice.
- Start slow, increase tempo gradually.
- Practice left hand separately — it's almost always weaker.
- Use contrary motion to make any exercise harder and more useful.
- Sit at the edge of the bench to let body weight transfer into the keys.
- Engage the upper back for tone production, not just the arms and hands.
- Don't tense up — especially avoid lifting the shoulders.
- Apply rhythmic variations to any exercise for evenness training.
- Chopin's natural hand position: thumb on E, then F#, G#, A#, C — the longer fingers (2, 3, 4) rest naturally on the black keys.

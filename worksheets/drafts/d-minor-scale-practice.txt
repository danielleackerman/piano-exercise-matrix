D MINOR — SCALE PRACTICE WORKSHEET
==========================================================
Key signature: One flat, B♭ · Relative minor of F major · 4 octaves


----------------------------------------------------------
PREAMBLE: RELATIVE VS. PARALLEL KEYS
----------------------------------------------------------

D minor is the relative minor of F major. Both keys share the same
key signature — one flat, B♭. A relative key shares the key
signature; a parallel key shares the root. The parallel minor of
D major is D minor; the parallel major of F major is F minor.
Knowing the difference matters when reading and analyzing music.


----------------------------------------------------------
SCALES
----------------------------------------------------------

01 — Scale, Natural Minor

Play D natural minor up and down across 4 octaves: D – E – F – G – A – B♭ – C – D.
The natural minor uses the same notes as F major starting from D.

  Tip: This is the foundation. The harmonic and melodic forms below
  alter specific degrees — practice the natural form first to feel
  the unaltered shape under the hand.


02 — Harmonic Minor

Raise the 7th degree: C becomes C♯. Scale: D – E – F – G – A – B♭ – C♯ – D.
The raised 7th gives the scale its characteristic minor sound and
creates a strong leading tone into the tonic.


03 — Melodic Minor

Ascending, raise both the 6th and 7th degrees: B♭ becomes B♮, C
becomes C♯. Ascending scale: D – E – F – G – A – B – C♯ – D.
Descending, return to the natural form: D – C – B♭ – A – G – F – E – D.

  Tip: The asymmetry between ascending and descending is what makes
  the melodic minor uniquely valuable for training.


04 — Scale in Thirds

Play the scale in intervals of a third — two notes a third apart,
played together or in succession.


05 — Scale in Tenths

Play the scale in tenths between the hands. A tenth feels like a
third, only an octave higher — the right hand plays one octave above
the third position.


06 — Scale in Sixths

Play the scale in sixths between the hands. The right hand starts on
D; the left hand starts a sixth below, on F. From there, both hands
move together through the scale.


----------------------------------------------------------
ARPEGGIOS & CHORDS — TONIC, D MINOR
----------------------------------------------------------

07 — Tonic Long Arpeggio

Play the tonic chord as a long arpeggio across the keyboard,
ascending and descending. Practice all three inversions:

  Root position    D – F – A – D
  1st inversion    F – A – D – F
  2nd inversion    A – D – F – A

Fingering, root position:
  RH: 1-2-3-1-2-3-1-2-3-1-2-3-5
  LH: 5-4-2-1-4-2-1-4-2-1-4-2-1

  Tip: Locate the harmonic function under the hand before playing.
  Recognizing tonic shape across inversions is the goal — not
  memorizing notes one at a time.


----------------------------------------------------------
ARPEGGIOS & CHORDS — DOMINANT, A MAJOR
----------------------------------------------------------

08 — Dominant Chord and Arpeggio

The dominant is built on the 5th degree of the scale: A. The dominant
chord in D minor is A major. The C♯ comes from the harmonic minor
scale.

Play each inversion as a solid chord with all notes struck together,
and as a long arpeggio across the keyboard:

  Root position    A – C♯ – E – A
  1st inversion    C♯ – E – A – C♯
  2nd inversion    E – A – C♯ – E


----------------------------------------------------------
ARPEGGIOS & CHORDS — SUBDOMINANT, G MINOR
----------------------------------------------------------

09 — Subdominant Chord and Arpeggio

The subdominant is built on the 4th degree of the scale: G. The
subdominant chord in D minor is G minor.

Play each inversion as a solid chord and as a long arpeggio across
the keyboard:

  Root position    G – B♭ – D – G
  1st inversion    B♭ – D – G – B♭
  2nd inversion    D – G – B♭ – D

  Tip: Most music — popular and classical — circles around three
  basic harmonic functions: tonic, dominant, subdominant.
  Recognizing them at the keyboard makes pieces easier to learn
  and memorize.


----------------------------------------------------------
VARIATIONS
----------------------------------------------------------

Contrary Motion

Any scale or arpeggio exercise can be played in contrary motion —
one hand ascending while the other descends simultaneously. Harder
than parallel motion and excellent brain training.


Accent & Rhythmic Variations

Apply to any scale or arpeggio exercise for fluidity and evenness:

  a — Accent every note: extra weight on each individual note.
  b — Accent every second note: grouped, rolling feel.
  c — Triplet groupings: organize the scale in groups of three.
  d — Groups of four: organize the scale in groups of four.
  e — Prolong one note per group: hold the 2nd or 3rd note slightly
      longer to build control.
  f — Groups of seven: the scale normally falls in groups of eight,
      so grouping in sevens shifts the accent and exposes unevenness.


Staccato

Any scale exercise can be played staccato — lift from each key rather
than connecting the notes. Treat it as a separate pass, not a
replacement for legato practice.


----------------------------------------------------------
GENERAL PRACTICE PRINCIPLES
----------------------------------------------------------

— Locate harmonic functions — tonic, dominant, subdominant — under
  the hand before playing through them
— Recognize chord shapes across all inversions, not just root
  position
— Practice scales in multiple intervals: octaves, thirds, sixths,
  tenths
— Practice the harmonic and melodic forms, not just the natural
  minor — the altered tones are where the technical value lives
— Memorize music in harmonic blocks, not note by note — thinking
  in chord shapes makes pieces easier to learn and retain
— Practice each hand separately before combining
— Apply scales knowledge directly to repertoire: identify the
  tonic, dominant, subdominant, and any altered chords in the
  music being learned

Scales
01 Scale — Parallel Motion

Play C major up and down with both hands moving in the same direction across 4 octaves.

Tip

Fingering: 4th finger on D, 3rd finger on A. Without black keys as landmarks, fingering is deceptively tricky. Start slow, prioritize evenness over speed.

02 Scale — Contrary Motion

Same scale, hands moving in opposite directions — one ascending, one descending simultaneously.

Tip

Harder than parallel motion. Excellent brain training. Add contrary motion to any exercise below for an extra challenge.

03 Scale in Thirds

Play the scale in intervals of a third — two notes a third apart, played together or in succession.

Tip

If the left hand feels awkward, isolate it. Practice left hand alone before combining.

04 Accent & Rhythmic Variations

Apply these to any scale exercise for fluidity and evenness:

a Accent every note — extra weight on each individual note.

b Accent every second note — grouped, rolling feel.

c Triplet groupings — organize the scale in groups of three.

05 Scale in Tenths (Triplets)

Play the scale in tenths (octave + third between hands) using triplet rhythm. Practice both ascending and descending.

Tip

Significantly harder. Descending reveals different weaknesses than ascending — don't skip it.

Arpeggios & Chords — Tonic
06 Arpeggios

Play C – E – G broken triads across 4 octaves.

Tip

Feel the natural weight of your arm dropping into the keys. Move in circular motions — stay grounded and free, not rigid.

07 Broken Chords

Play tonic chord C – E – G in broken form (root position) across the keyboard.

Tip

If uncomfortable, practice left hand separately first. The left hand naturally wants to lean right — be aware of this pull.

08 Chords

Play solid chords — all notes struck together.

Warm-up method: First play just the octave (C to C). Then add the middle voices (E, G) one at a time. This activates the inner fingers while the octave frame stays stable.

Technique

Lock in the octave feel — octaves always feel the same physically. Activate your upper back, not just the arms. Arms alone produce a pushed, thin sound. Engage the back for a heavy, beautiful tone. Don't lift your shoulders.

09 Long Arpeggios

Extended arpeggios across the full keyboard. Root position, 1st inversion, 2nd inversion. Add contrary motion.

Practice method: Separate hands first — this exposes weaknesses you hide when both hands play together.

Technique

Great exercise for thumb crossing. Move the center of your body with your hands instead of reaching with arms alone. Sit on the edge of the bench — sitting too far back sends your weight into the chair, not the keyboard.

Dominant — G7
10 G7 Exercises (Arpeggios, Broken Chords, Chords, Long Arpeggios)

Repeat exercises 6–9 using G – B – D – F (V7 of C major). Play through all inversions: root, 1st, 2nd, 3rd.

Warm-up: Octave first, then add three middle voices — even more stretch than tonic.

Tip

Bigger hand stretch (4 notes). Don't play these at the start of warm-up. Some intervals are close (F–G), then a bigger gap — physically awkward. Apply rhythmic variations from Exercise 4.

Finishing
11 Diminished — B dim

Built on the 7th degree: B – D – F (all minor thirds). Apply the same arpeggio and chord exercises. To be expanded in later sessions.

12 Chromatic Scale

Play the chromatic scale starting from C across the full keyboard. A good way to close out your session.

General Practice Principles
Always play in 4 octaves for serious practice
Start slow, increase tempo gradually
Practice left hand separately — it's almost always weaker
Use contrary motion to make any exercise harder
Sit at the edge of the bench for weight transfer
Engage the upper back for tone, not just the arms
Don't tense up — avoid lifting the shoulders
Apply rhythmic variations for evenness training
Chopin's Natural Hand Position

The perfect position places the thumb on E, with the longer 2nd, 3rd, and 4th fingers resting naturally on the black keys.

E — F♯ — G♯ — A♯ — C
